-----------------------

The Crooked Rook

-----------------------

This is a (weak) chess engine that I created a few years ago to learn more about how they work. It was simpler than I expected, but only thanks to the wealth of resources/tutorials available. Lots of the specific algorithm implementation (especially related to positional scoring) are nothing original, but tons of fun to implement.

I was also heavily inspired by a few throw-away lines in the show Lost about a computer terminal chess program that apparently couldn't be trusted, because it could cheat. There may even be ways that the Crooked Rook will turn things to his advantage if the game turns south...

-----------------------

How does a chess engine work?

-----------------------

Each player has a hidden "score" that they are trying to increase. The engine will evaluate moves in an attempt to increase the score, or minimize loses.

The score's foundation is the total value of all remaining pieces. For example...

Pawn value: 100

Rook value: 500

King value: 20000

So, eliminating enmey pieces is an obvious way to increase the score, but positional scoring is also an important influence.

In simplest terms, each piece has a reference guide on what positions are ideal.

Here's an example of the positional scoring system for the Black Knight...

var black_knight_eval_early =

[[-50, -40, -30, -30, -30, -30, -40, -50],

[-40, -20, 0, 0, 0, 0, -20, -40],

[-30, 5, 10, 15, 15, 10, 5, -30],

[-30, 0, 15, 20, 20, 15, 0, -30],

[-30, 5, 15, 20, 20, 15, 5, -30],

[-30, 0, 10, 15, 15, 10, 0, -30],

[-40, 20, 0, -5, -5, 0, 20, -40],

[-50, -40, -30, -30, -30, -30, -40, -50]];

I'm certainly not skilled at using a Knight, but general advice would be to keep it near the center where its reach across even two moves can be devastating, vs a weak position in the far corners. You can see how that translates to this positional scoring reference.

Compare the Knight with the Black King...

var black_king_eval_early =

[[ 20, 30, 10, 0, 0, 10, 30, 20],

[ 20, 20, 0, 0, 0, 0, 20, 20],

[-10, -20, -20, -20, -20, -20, -20, -10],

[-20, -30, -30, -40, -40, -30, -30, -20],

[-30, -40, -40, -50, -50, -40, -40, -30],

[-30, -40, -40, -50, -50, -40, -40, -30],

[-30, -40, -40, -50, -50, -40, -40, -30],

[-30, -40, -40, -50, -50, -40, -40, -30]];

There is a little more side specific logic at play, albeit simple, for the King. Basically, don't just walk over to the other side, brother.

The true power of these guidelines is that they would ideally be dynamic. The Crooked Rook is an incredibly simple engine that never moves beyond early game strategy. But, pieces could have different goals for different stages of play, and could shift their positional value on based on other pieces (where the king is for example). The possibilities may not be quite as large as the potential moves in a Chess game, but certainly still far more than what is represented by this project.

-----------------------

But, how does strategy develop?

-----------------------

You would certainly think that the engine would always prioritize captures, but at higher difficulties, the engine is not just comparing the current state of the board. It will evaluate additional permutations across future moves. Two fantastic moves mean nothing if they lead to the Queen being completely trapped.

It is a combination of all these factors that make it feel like the engine has a genuine strategy and understanding of the game.
